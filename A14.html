Chapter 13: Hydrocarbons

Understanding about hydrocarbons

Definition

Hydrocarbons are compounds composed of carbon and hydrogen. Key fuels like petrol, kerosene, and CNG are hydrocarbons or mixtures of them.

Sources

  • Petroleum & Natural Gas: Major sources of aliphatic hydrocarbons.
  • Coal: Main source of aromatic hydrocarbons.
  • Petroleum: Oil trapped in rocks; natural gas contains methane, ethane, propane, and butane.

Classification of Hydrocarbons

Acyclic (Aliphatic)

  • Alkanes (Paraffins): CnH2n+2, sp³ hybridized, chemically unreactive.
  • Alkenes (Olefins): CnH2n, contain a double bond, sp² hybridized.
  • Alkynes: CnH2n-2, contain a triple bond, sp hybridized.

Cyclic (Carbocyclic)

  • Alicyclic: Contain carbon rings (e.g., cycloalkanes).
  • Aromatic: Contain a benzene ring.

Alkanes

General Formula: CnH2n+2

Properties: Non-polar, low boiling points, and colorless liquids or solids.

Preparation

  • Wurtz reaction
  • Grignard reagent
  • From unsaturated hydrocarbons

Reactions

  • Combustion: Complete oxidation to CO₂ and H₂O
  • Halogenation (e.g., chlorination)
  • Iodination (reversible with oxidizing agents)
  • Fluorination (explosive)
  • Substitution reactions (e.g., nitration, sulfonation)

Alkenes

General Formula: CnH2n

Properties: Unsaturated, contain a double bond.

Reactions

  • Addition: Hydration, halogenation, hydrogenation (with Lindlar's catalyst for cis-alkenes)
  • Electrophilic Addition: Follows Markovnikov's rule (except with peroxides)
  • Oxidation: Hydroboration, ozonolysis

Alkynes

General Formula: CnH2n-2

Properties: Contain a triple bond, sp hybridized.

Reactions

  • Hydrogenation: Can be controlled to form alkenes
  • Addition of Halogens and Hydrogen Halides
  • Hydration: Acid catalyzed addition of water

Aromatic Hydrocarbons

Benzene: Six carbon atoms in a hexagonal ring, with delocalized π electrons.

Aromaticity: Follows Hückel’s rule (4n+2 π electrons).

Preparation

  • From ethyne
  • Decarboxylation of aromatic acids
  • Reduction of phenol

Reactions

  • Electrophilic Substitution: Includes halogenation, nitration, sulfonation

Physical Properties

  • Non-polar
  • Colorless
  • Immiscible with water

Key Reactions and Mechanisms

  • Halogenation (Alkanes): Follows free radical mechanism
  • Hydrogenation (Alkenes & Alkynes): Can be selective (cis or trans)
  • Polymerization: Alkynes can form linear or cyclic polymers
