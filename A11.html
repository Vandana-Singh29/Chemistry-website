Chapter 10: The s-Block Elements

Important Properties, Compounds, and Biological Importance of Alkali and Alkaline Earth Metals

Important Points

Groups 1 and 2 are the s-block elements in the Periodic Table.

  • Group 1: Alkali metals (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr)
  • Group 2: Alkaline earth metals (Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra) - except for Beryllium, the other elements are known as alkaline earth metals.

Physical Properties

Group 1: Alkali Metals

  • Large Atomic Radii: The atomic radii of alkali metals are the largest in their respective periods. They increase as we move down the group.
  • Large Ionic Radii: The ionic radii increase as we move down the group, due to the addition of a new energy shell with each succeeding element.
  • Low Ionization Enthalpy: Alkali metals have low ionization enthalpies because the s-electron is loosely held, and these enthalpies decrease as we move down the group.
  • Hydration Enthalpy: Hydration enthalpy decreases with increasing ionic radii. Li has the highest hydration enthalpy, while Cs has the lowest.
  • Oxidation State: Alkali metals exhibit a +1 oxidation state in their compounds and are highly electropositive. Electropositivity increases from Li to Cs.
  • Metallic Character: Metallic character increases down the group.
  • Melting and Boiling Points: Melting and boiling points of alkali metals are low and decrease with increasing atomic number.
  • Nature of Bonds: Alkali metals form ionic bonds. The ionic character increases down the group.
  • Flame Colouration: Each alkali metal imparts a characteristic colour to the flame.
  • Photoelectric Effect: Alkali metals (except Li) exhibit the photoelectric effect.

Chemical Features of Alkali Metals

  • Reducing Character: Alkali metals exhibit increasing reducing power as we move down the group. (Li < Na < K < Rb < Cs)
  • Reaction with Hydrogen: Alkali metals react with hydrogen at high temperatures to form crystalline hydrides that are ionic and have high melting points.
  • Reaction with Oxygen: Alkali metals react with limited oxygen to form normal oxides (M₂O), where M is Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs.
  • Reaction with Halogens: Alkali metals react with halogens to form ionic halides. Reactivity increases down the group.
  • Reaction with Water: Alkali metals react with water to release hydrogen gas.
  • Solubility in Liquid Ammonia: Alkali metals dissolve in liquid ammonia, giving deep blue solutions that are conductive.
  • Reaction with Sulphur and Phosphorus: Alkali metals react with sulfur and phosphorus on heating to form sulfides and phosphides.

Diagonal Relationship between Li and Mg

Li resembles Mg because of similarities in atomic size and properties:

  • Both are hard.
  • LiOH and Mg(OH)₂ are weak bases.
  • Carbonates of Li and Mg decompose to produce oxides and CO₂.
  • Both react with nitrogen to form ionic nitrides.
  • Nitrates of Li and Mg decompose on heating to give oxides.
  • Neither forms solid bicarbonates.
  • LiCl and MgCl₂ are soluble in ethanol due to covalent character.
  • The hydroxides, bicarbonates, and fluorides of Li and Mg are sparingly soluble in water.

Biological Importance of Na and K

Sodium Ions (Na⁺)

  • Participate in nerve signal transmission.
  • Regulate the flow of water across cell membranes.
  • Aid in the transport of sugars and amino acids into cells.

Potassium Ions (K⁺)

  • Most abundant cations inside cell fluids.
  • Activate enzymes and are involved in glucose oxidation to produce ATP.
  • Help transmit nerve signals along with sodium ions.
  • The functional features of nerve cells depend on the sodium-potassium ion gradient established in the cell.

Group 2: Alkaline Earth Metals

  • Atomic Radii: The atomic radii of alkaline earth metals are large, but smaller than alkali metals in the same period. These radii increase down the group.
  • Ionic Radii: These metals form divalent ions, and their size increases down the group.
  • Ionization Enthalpy: The ionization enthalpies are lower than group 1 but decrease down the group.
  • Hydration Enthalpy: Hydration enthalpy decreases as the metal ion size increases (Be²⁺ > Mg²⁺ > Ca²⁺ > Sr²⁺ > Ba²⁺).
  • Oxidation State: All the members exhibit a +2 oxidation state and form divalent cations (M²⁺).
  • Electronegativity: Alkaline earth metals have slightly higher electronegativity than alkali metals.
  • Metallic Character: Alkaline earth metals have stronger metallic bonds compared to alkali metals in the same period.
  • Melting and Boiling Points: Higher than alkali metals in the same period.
  • Flame Colouration: Except for beryllium and magnesium, the other elements impart characteristic colours to flames (e.g., Ca - Brick Red, Sr - Crimson).

Uses of Some Important Compounds

  • Caustic Soda (NaOH): Used in the soap, paper, textile, and petroleum industries.
  • Sodium Carbonate (Na₂CO₃): Used in glass, soap, paper, textile, paint, and dye industries.
  • Quick Lime (CaO): Used in cement, glass, calcium carbide, sugar purification, and softening hard water.
  • Limestone (CaCO₃): Used in building, manufacture of quick lime, and in the Solvay process.
  • Cement: Used in concrete, reinforced concrete, and construction of buildings and bridges.
  • Plaster of Paris (CaSO₄·½H₂O): Used in making molds, statues, and surgical bandages.

Biological Importance of Ca and Mg

  • Magnesium (Mg²⁺): Essential for ATP utilization in animals and present in chlorophyll in plants.
  • Calcium (Ca²⁺):
    • Important for muscle contraction, nerve impulse transmission, and blood clotting.
    • Regulates heartbeat.
    • Involved in the formation of bones and teeth.
