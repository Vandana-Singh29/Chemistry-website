Environmental Chemistry Overview

Introduction

Study of chemical processes in the environment—origin, transport, reactions, effects, and fate of chemical species.

Environmental Pollution

Pollutant: Substance causing pollution (solid, liquid, or gas).

Atmospheric Pollution: Includes tropospheric and stratospheric pollution.

The ozone layer absorbs ~99.5% of UV rays.

Tropospheric Pollution

Gaseous Pollutants: CO, SO₂, NOₓ, hydrocarbons, ozone, H₂S.

Particulates: Dust, mist, smoke, smog, fumes.

Global Warming & Greenhouse Effect

Heat is trapped by gases like CO₂, CH₄, O₃, CFCs, and H₂O vapour.

Greenhouse analogy: Visible light enters, infrared rays get trapped.

Acid Rain

Occurs when pH < 5.6 due to acids like H₂SO₄, HNO₃, HCl.

Effects: Harms crops, aquatic life, buildings, and causes health issues.

Smog

Classical Smog: Cool, humid, reducing (contains SO₂).

Photochemical Smog: Warm, dry, oxidizing (contains ozone, PAN).

Ozone Hole

Caused by CFCs and NO.

Leads to UV radiation reaching Earth, causing skin issues and reduced aquatic productivity.

Water Pollution

Sources: Sewage, industry, agriculture, thermal and radioactive waste.

Eutrophication: Excess nutrients → plant overgrowth → oxygen depletion.

BOD (Biochemical Oxygen Demand): Amount of oxygen required by bacteria to decompose organic matter.

Soil Pollution

Primarily caused by overuse of pesticides, insecticides, and herbicides.

Green Chemistry

Designing chemical processes to minimize environmental harm.

  • Using CO₂ instead of perchloroethylene in dry cleaning.
  • Using H₂O₂ instead of chlorine for paper bleaching.
  • Environment-friendly ethanal production methods.
