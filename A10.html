Hydrogen

Key Properties, Preparation, and Uses of Hydrogen

Position in the Periodic Table

Hydrogen is the first element, with unique properties that place it in both Group I (alkali metals) and Group VII (halogens).

  • It shares characteristics with alkali metals (e.g., single electron in outer shell).
  • It also shares characteristics with halogens (e.g., diatomic nature and ability to form anions).

Discovery & Occurrence

  • Discovered by Henry Cavendish in 1766 and named by Lavoisier.
  • Found as a diatomic gas and is the 9th most abundant element in Earth's crust.

Preparation of Dihydrogen

Methods to prepare dihydrogen include:

  • Reaction with metals (e.g., Na, K, Al).
  • Electrolysis of water.
  • Reaction with acids (e.g., HCl).
  • Commercial methods like Bosch and Lane's processes.
  • Pure hydrogen is obtained via methods like electrolysis of barium hydroxide.

Physical Properties

  • Colourless, tasteless, odourless gas.
  • Slightly soluble in water.
  • Highly combustible.

Chemical Properties

  • Stable but reacts at high temperatures (above 2000 K) to form hydrogen atoms.
  • Reacts with metals to form hydrides, and with non-metals (e.g., halogens) to form compounds.
  • Hydrogenation of unsaturated hydrocarbons to form saturated ones.

Uses

  • As a reducing agent.
  • In hydrogenation of oils, as rocket fuel, and in synthetic petrol production.
  • In ammonia synthesis (Haber process), HCl production, and margarine manufacturing.

Forms of Hydrogen

  • Atomic Hydrogen: Produced by dissociating hydrogen molecules; very reactive.
  • Nascent Hydrogen: More reactive form produced in chemical reactions.
  • Ortho and Para Hydrogen: Differ in nuclear spin; the ratio varies with temperature.

Hydrides

Hydrogen forms various hydrides (ionic, metallic, and molecular) with metals and non-metals.

  • Saline hydrides are formed by alkali metals.
  • Metallic hydrides are formed by transition and f-block metals.
  • Molecular hydrides are formed by p-block elements.

Isotopes

Hydrogen has three isotopes:

  • Protium (H): Most common isotope.
  • Deuterium (D): Contains one proton and one neutron.
  • Tritium (T): Contains one proton and two neutrons.

Isotopes differ in mass and some physical properties, such as the reaction rate between H₂ and Cl₂ being faster than between D₂ and Cl₂.
